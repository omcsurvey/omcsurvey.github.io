Hepatitis-B Knowledge, Attitudes, and Vaccination Status Among Medical Students

A Comprehensive Observational Study and Research

Background Objectives Hypotheses Methodology Outcomes Ethics

Background

Hepatitis B (HBV) is a significant public health concern worldwide, especially in the developing world, including India. Medical students are at a higher risk due to their encounter with blood and other body fluids during clinical exposure. Although vaccination against HBV has been proven to be the most effective preventive method, there is still room for improvement of its uptake among medical students, which is influenced by their awareness, attitude, and accessibility. The knowledge of these factors is crucial to the development of interventions that can be tailor-made to increase the rate of vaccination.

Aim

The present research is aimed at providing a comprehensive evaluation of Indian medical students' knowledge of Hepatitis B, as well as their attitudes toward it, and to determine the impact of these factors on their vaccination status. The purpose of the study is to identify the barriers to vaccination in order to develop evidence-based policies and educational programs to improve the vaccine coverage of future health professionals.

Research Objectives

Primary Objective

To observe the relationship between medical students’ knowledge of Hepatitis-B, their attitudes toward vaccination, and their actual vaccination status? And to find the correlation between it.

Specific Objectives

  • Knowledge Assessment
  • Attitudinal Measures
  • Vaccination Status Evaluation
  • Influence Of Demography, Knowledge, Personal and other Factors on Vaccination

Research Hypotheses

Knowledge and Vaccination:

H1

Higher knowledge about Hepatitis-B is associated with higher vaccination rates

H2

Misunderstandings about hepatitis B transmission and prevention relate to attitudes. Even among well-informed individuals, higher vaccination rates were linked to positive attitudes and greater knowledge.

Attitudinal Influence:

H3

Positive vaccine attitudes directly correlate with vaccination status

H4

Unwillingness for vaccination due to negative perceptions or individual circumstances is pertinently linked disapproving immunization uptake

Demographic Variability:

H5

Clinical-year students have better knowledge and vaccination rates

H6

Female medical students may have different attitudes toward vaccination when compared to male peers, influencing uptake

Barriers to Vaccination:

H7

Systemic barriers(accessibility) affect vaccination program accessibility

Methodology

Study Design

Cross-sectional mixed-methods design integrating a quantitative survey (structured questionnaire) and qualitative interviews to evaluate Knowledge, Attitudes, and Behavior (KAB) dynamics toward Hepatitis-B vaccination.

Study Population

Medical students from different institutions of India were used in the studies a Sample size of approx 384 students was calculated but we get only 168 response

Data Collection

Structured questionnaire with demographic, knowledge, attitude and status assessments was made using HTML/JS/CSS and was hosted and distributed among medical students of India using social media

Data Analysis

Descriptive statistics, Visual Charts, correlation analysis, Machine model(ML), and logistic regression using Python and Data science

Expected Outcomes

This study anticipates elucidating the complex interplay between Hepatitis-B knowledge, attitudes, and vaccination behavior among medical students. By analyzing demographic, cognitive, and systemic factors, the research aims to identify actionable insights to improve vaccination programs. The expected outcomes are structured below, aligned with the tested hypotheses:

Knowledge as a Predictor of Vaccination

H1 Validation: Confirmed

Based on the Visual analysis there was found to be good corellation between Knowledge score and Vaccination status. Althrough Statistical analysis demonstrates a moderate positive correlation (r = 0.41, p < 0.001) between Hepatitis-B knowledge scores and vaccination status. Logistic regression showed Strong corellation confirms that each 1-point increase in knowledge score raises vaccination odds by 55% (OR = 1.55, p = 0.000), validating the hypothesis.

H2 Rejection: Partial Insight

Visual analysis don't show much difference in graph of Vaccinated and non-Vaccinated students by Misconception count. Contrary to expectations, misconception counts showed no significant association (p = 0.106) with vaccination status. While knowledge remained a strong predictor (p = 0.000), misconceptions alone did not meaningfully influence vaccination decisions.

Attitudinal Determinants of Vaccine Uptake

H3 Validation: Strong Support

The Visual analysis(box plot and violin plot) show that mean attitude score was higher for Vaccinated students. A statistically significant difference (t = 6.57, p = 5.6×10⁻⁸) in attitude scores exists between vaccinated and non-vaccinated groups. Logistic regression reveals a 1.56-unit increase in log-odds of vaccination per attitude score point (p = 3.5×10⁻¹⁰), confirming attitudes directly drive vaccination behavior.

H4 Rejection: Limited Cultural Impact

Visual analysis of Cultural barrier among vaccinated ad non-Vaccinated students don't revealed any big changes. Numerical analysis of Cultural barriers showed no significant association (p = 0.116) with vaccination status. While non-vaccinated students reported more cultural concerns, multivariate analysis found these barriers did not independently predict vaccination decisions.

Demographic Disparities in Vaccination

H5 Validation: Academic Year Matters

Althrough the data was highly immbalanced for higher academic years but in Visual analysis it was able to show significant increase in knowedge score as Academic Year increases. And since it was indirectly showing corellation with Vaccination satuseven in immbalanced data. Numerical analysis revealed a significant chi-square association (χ² = 53.67, p = 0.000) exists between academic year and vaccination status. Clinical-year students exhibit higher knowledge scores and vaccination rates, with each academic year increasing vaccination odds by 32% (OR = 1.32, p = 0.001).

H6 Rejection: Gender Neutrality

Visual analysis showed less/no difference between the Male and female Vaccinated and non-Vaccinated students. No significant gender differences in vaccination rates or attitudes were observed. Both male and female students showed comparable vaccine uptake patterns, suggesting gender is not a determinant in this population.

Structural Barriers to Immunization

H7 Partial Validation: Accessibility Challenges

Visual analysis shows that there is significant impact and presence of the Barriers in the Institutions and affect on the Vaccination status. While financial barriers showed minimal impact(on non-Vaccinated students), Comapared to Vaccinated students who cited Financial and Scheduling as significant deterrents. Non-vaccinated students disproportionately cited Communication and Institutional accessibility as a critical barrier.

Implications for Public Health

The findings underscore that knowledge enhancement programs and attitude-focused interventions should be prioritized for pre-clinical students. Addressing institutional, communication, financial and access barriers could further improve vaccination rates. Contrary to assumptions, cultural beliefs and gender disparities play limited roles in this population.
